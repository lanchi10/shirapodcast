Research by Dr. Shira Ramot, supervised by Prof. Orna Tal

Study Overview

This 2021 research explores perceptions of risk, benefit, and trust towards three medical technologies:

  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis (LASIK)
  • Covid-19 Vaccine

Comparing healthcare workers (HCWs) and the general public.

Key Findings

  • All technologies: high benefit, low risk
  • HCWs vs public: Different risk perceptions for MRI and LASIK
  • Covid-19 vaccine: Similar risk perception in both groups
  • HCWs: Higher subjective knowledge and familiarity
  • Trust in Ministry of Health: Negatively correlated with perceived risk for MRI and Covid-19 vaccine

Detailed Observations

  • Public: Higher perceived risk and benefit for MRI
  • HCWs: Higher perceived health risks for LASIK
  • Covid-19 vaccine: Similar risk perceptions (possibly due to novelty)
  • Women: Generally higher perceived risks for Covid-19 vaccine and LASIK
  • Flu vaccine willingness: Negatively correlated with perceived risk of Covid-19 vaccine and MRI

Information Sources

Medical sources: Most reliable for both groups

  • HCWs: Scientific articles and personal experience
  • Public: Family/friends and personal experience

Religious leaders/authority figures: Lowest credibility

Implications

  • Provide accurate risk information
  • Address concerns of HCWs and public
  • Strengthen trust in health authorities
  • Tailor communication strategies
  • Recognize HCWs' personal concerns
  • Leverage trust in medical sources

Conclusion

Understanding risk perceptions is crucial for effective communication and technology acceptance in healthcare. This study provides insights into factors influencing perceptions among healthcare professionals and the public, emphasizing the need for targeted education and communication strategies.
